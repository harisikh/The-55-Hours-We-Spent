artifacts/ — the real thing.

This is not a photograph of a memory. Every section before the ending asks what
happened; this one answers what remains. It is an object that exists right now,
and the ending rests it on the desk as itself rather than showing a picture of
it. That is this piece's grammar for a real object that was actually kept.

sunflower.png
  Two dried sunflowers out of the bouquet she gave him. From IMG_8248.JPG, a
  flat-lay of them on the hessian the bouquet was wrapped in. There are two of
  them in the photograph, so there are two of them here; nothing was entitled to
  throw one away to make the composition tidier.

What was done to it, and only this: the background was removed and the alpha
edge softened, EXIF orientation corrected, and the result resized. Nothing was
graded, retouched, relit, straightened or rotated. The pixels are his photograph.

perfume.png was here and has been deleted. It was the perfume she gave him, cut
out of IMG_8249.jpg, and it stood beside the sunflowers on the closing page. It
came off that page because it was a photograph of a glossy bottle standing under
a different light, which forced the flowers to be presented as a print too and
turned the ending into two pictures of things rather than the things. The bottle
did come back — as an impression in the paper on the page that names it, drawn
rather than photographed. See assets/marks/perfume.png. Nothing in artifacts/ is
a picture of an object; that is the whole rule, and one file could not keep it.

keepsake.png is gone. It was a placeholder — a dashed square and a crosshair —
standing in for a decision that had deliberately not been made yet. It has been
made. Its contract said "roughly square, photographed flat and lit evenly", and
these two are neither square nor a matched pair of studio shots. That is the
correct outcome: the real objects decided the ending, not the other way round.

Cutting them out was not free. The hessian shows through the gaps between dried
petals and it is the same mid-grey as the shadows between them, and the perfume
is a mirror that reflects a dark room and therefore reads as background to every
brightness rule there is. Both mattes were solved with seeded segmentation and
judged by looking at them on the desk, which was the only test that mattered.
